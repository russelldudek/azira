My operating thesis

The Chief of Staff should build the company’s decision nervous system—not become its central switchboard.

Clarity is a system.

The highest-leverage Chief of Staff is not the person who knows everything happening everywhere. It is the person who builds the mechanisms that help leaders see what matters, decide faster, assign ownership, expose risk, and learn from outcomes.

My operating principle: influence through useful structure, not process for its own sake.

01 / PRIORITIES

Make tradeoffs explicit.

One visible set of company priorities, outcome measures, owners, assumptions, and resource tensions.

02 / DECISIONS

Reduce decision debt.

Clarify the decision, owner, deadline, evidence required, dissent, and what changes after the decision.

03 / COMMITMENTS

Translate discussion into motion.

Every executive decision leaves the room with accountable owners, dates, dependencies, and escalation triggers.

04 / VISIBILITY

Build one source of truth.

Revenue, profitability, customer health, strategic initiatives, operating performance, and risk in one executive view.

05 / LEARNING LOOP

Close the loop from outcome back to assumption.

QBRs and business reviews should reveal which assumptions held, which failed, and what the company changes next—not merely narrate last quarter.

Four operating loops

Where I would focus the work.

Each loop starts with a calibrated question because the wrong operating system can create more drag than the problem it was meant to solve.

“What is taking CEO attention today that should become a repeatable company mechanism tomorrow?”

The role should increase the CEO’s leverage: frame decisions, prepare evidence, pressure-test assumptions, sequence priorities, and protect attention without isolating the CEO from reality.

Decision briefs

Context, options, evidence, tradeoffs, recommendation, decision owner and deadline.

Priority architecture

Company outcomes, strategic initiatives, dependencies, owner confidence and resource tension.

Board readiness

Strategy narrative, progress evidence, risk visibility, metrics integrity and follow-through.

“Which executive meetings create decisions—and which merely create more meetings?”

Design a leadership cadence around the work: weekly decision forum, monthly operating review, QBR, annual planning, and focused off-sites with clear outputs and ownership.

Before

Pre-reads, explicit decisions, unresolved tensions, trusted metrics and clear asks.

During

Facilitation that keeps debate productive, captures decisions, and surfaces dissent.

After

Commitments, owners, due dates, dependencies, escalation conditions and visible closure.

“What would have to be visible for the ELT to know the company is off-plan two weeks earlier?”

Create an executive performance architecture that connects revenue, profitability, pipeline quality, customer health, delivery, product adoption, strategic initiatives, and risk.

Lead indicators

Early signals that reveal future pressure before financial outcomes arrive.

Decision thresholds

Metrics linked to explicit actions, not dashboards that merely glow red.

Narrative discipline

What changed, why, what we learned, and what leaders will do differently.

“Where is high-value work currently losing velocity at a functional boundary?”

Lead a small number of cross-functional missions where coordination failure is materially slowing revenue, customer value, product launches, profitability, or strategic progress.

Mission charter

Outcome, owner, decision rights, milestones, economics, dependencies and stop conditions.

War-room rhythm

Short, evidence-based operating cadence focused on constraints and decisions.

Exit into system

The Chief of Staff should leave behind a stronger process, owner and metric—not permanent dependency.

Executive cockpit

The CEO’s week should not be a scavenger hunt.

A useful source of truth combines business health, strategic progress, decision needs, and emerging risk in one place.

Growth

Revenue & pipeline quality

Bookings, conversion, concentration, forecast confidence, expansion and channel or partner contribution.

Value

Profitability & unit economics

Gross margin, delivery mix, cost-to-serve, customer economics and initiative benefits.

Trust

Customer health

Adoption, retention risk, time-to-value, escalations, service commitments and strategic accounts.

Motion

Strategic initiative health

Milestones, dependencies, blocked decisions, resource tension, owner confidence and outcome risk.

Executive review question

What needs a decision, not an update?

  • Top three decisions required this week
  • Cross-functional risks without a clear owner
  • Metrics outside agreed decision thresholds
  • Initiatives consuming attention without expected value
  • Customer or market signals that challenge current assumptions
